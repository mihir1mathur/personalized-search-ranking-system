WHICH METHOD PERFORMS BETTER?
----------------------------------------------------------------
On NDCG@10 (our headline ranking metric), the better method is: BM25.

WHY BM25 OFTEN BEATS TF-IDF:
  - Term-frequency saturation: BM25 stops rewarding a word after
    it appears a few times, so 'keyword stuffed' products do not
    dominate. Plain TF-IDF keeps rewarding repeats more linearly.
  - Document-length normalization: BM25 fairly handles long product
    descriptions, so a short, on-topic title can still win over a
    long, loosely-related description.
  - These two ideas usually make BM25 rank the truly relevant
    products a little higher, which NDCG rewards.

WHERE TF-IDF STILL WORKS WELL:
  - Short, clean documents where length normalization matters less.
  - When you need cosine similarity vectors for other tasks
    (clustering, simple similarity, feeding another model).
  - As a fast, easy-to-explain baseline that is built into scikit-learn.

These keyword baselines are the bar that future dense-retrieval and
transformer models (later weeks) must beat.

----------------------------------------------------------------
LIMITATIONS OF KEYWORD SEARCH
----------------------------------------------------------------
BM25 is strong, but it is NOT sufficient on its own. Both BM25 and
TF-IDF share the same core weakness: they match WORDS, not MEANING.

  - Exact-word dependence: a product is only matched if it literally
    contains the query words. If the shopper and the product use
    different words for the same thing, the product can be missed.
  - Abbreviation problems: "pjs" is not recognized as "pajamas",
    "TV" is not recognized as "television".
  - Synonym problems: "cord" is treated as unrelated to "cable",
    "laptop" as unrelated to "notebook computer".
  - Semantic understanding limitations: keyword scoring cannot grasp
    intent or themes (e.g. "portable", "tankless vs tank", or a
    "horror" theme) -- it only counts shared words and discounts by
    word rarity and document length.

WHY BM25 IS STRONG BUT NOT SUFFICIENT:
  BM25 wins this week because, when the wording matches, its
  saturation and length-normalization rank the right products well.
  But when the wording differs (synonyms, abbreviations, spelling
  mistakes, paraphrases), BM25 has no way to know two different words
  mean the same thing. See results/week1_failure_cases.txt for five
  real queries where this breaks down. Closing this "meaning gap" is
  the motivation for Week 2's embedding-based semantic retrieval.
